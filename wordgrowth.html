by Szu Yu Chen

Data

Wordbank, an open database of children's vocabulary development, provides a way for people to generate analyses of vocabulary growth for different groups of children. Through its dataset, we can answer question like, "how many kids are reported to pronounce mommy or alligator at 16 months?" Out of my own curiosity, I explore some interesting "words" that are earliest or latest learned in English.
I use gradient colors to represent the percentage of kids producing certain words.

For example, 93% of 16-month-old kids can pronounce "mommy", which is represented by an orange color. However, only around 3% of kids at 16 months can pronounce "alligator", which is represented by a lighter color.

No! No! No!

64% of kids at 16 months learn how to say no, while only 35% of them learn to say yes.












And then they say yes.

As they grow older, they start saying yes. (Finally!!!)












Kids learn love, but not hate.

More than half of the kids at 23 months learn to say "love". Around 90% of 30-month-old kids can pronounce "love" as well. Only 27% of kids at 30 months learn to say "hate."












More penis
Less vagina

37% of kids who are 30-month-old learn to say "vagina" while more than 54% of them can say "penis."

PS: The composition of the database for English(American) contains 3,203 females and 3,408 males.
